# Singapore Food Waste Context and Regulations

## 1. Singapore Food Waste Statistics

### Overview (2023 Data)

- **Total food waste generated**: 755,000 tonnes (2023)
- **Food waste as % of total waste**: ~11% of total waste stream
- **Recycling rate for food waste**: 18% (2023) — up from 16% in 2022
- **Food waste recycled**: ~136,000 tonnes
- **Food waste disposed**: ~619,000 tonnes (incinerated at waste-to-energy plants)

### 5-Year Trend

| Year | Food Waste (tonnes) | Recycling Rate |
| ---- | ------------------- | -------------- |
| 2019 | 744,000             | 18%            |
| 2020 | 665,000             | 19%            |
| 2021 | 677,000             | 19%            |
| 2022 | 739,000             | 16%            |
| 2023 | 755,000             | 18%            |

Food waste has rebounded post-COVID and continues to rise, making it a growing concern for Singapore's waste management infrastructure.

### Breakdown by Source

| Source                          | % of Food Waste | Tonnes (est.) |
| ------------------------------- | --------------- | ------------- |
| Households                      | 40%             | ~302,000      |
| F&B establishments              | 17%             | ~128,000      |
| Food retail/supermarkets        | 13%             | ~98,000       |
| Food manufacturing              | 15%             | ~113,000      |
| Others (institutions, catering) | 15%             | ~113,000      |

**Key insight for our platform**: F&B + food retail = ~30% of food waste (~226,000 tonnes/year). This is our addressable supply — merchants in these categories generate surplus that can be redistributed.

## 2. F&B Industry Landscape in Singapore

### Market Size

- **Total F&B establishments**: ~14,000-15,000 (including hawker stalls, food courts, restaurants, cafes, bakeries)
- **Restaurants and cafes**: ~7,000-8,000
- **Hawker stalls**: ~6,000 (in ~110 hawker centers)
- **Food courts**: ~600
- **Bakeries**: ~500
- **Supermarkets**: ~400+

### Common Causes of Food Waste (F&B)

| Cause                    | % of F&B Waste | Description                                   |
| ------------------------ | -------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Overproduction           | 34%            | Preparing more than customer demand           |
| Spoilage/expiration      | 21%            | Ingredients and prepared items past freshness |
| Customer plate waste     | 18%            | Food ordered but not consumed                 |
| Trim/preparation waste   | 15%            | Food prep byproducts                          |
| Over-ordering/purchasing | 12%            | Excess ingredient procurement                 |

### Surplus Patterns by Establishment Type

| Type          | Typical Surplus                  | Timing                                     | Volume                      |
| ------------- | -------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ | --------------------------- |
| Bakeries      | Bread, pastries, cakes           | End of day (7-9pm)                         | 10-30% of daily production  |
| Restaurants   | Prepared dishes, ingredients     | After lunch (2-3pm), after dinner (9-10pm) | 5-15% of daily prep         |
| Cafes         | Sandwiches, salads, baked goods  | End of day (5-7pm)                         | 10-20% of daily stock       |
| Supermarkets  | Near-expiry produce, dairy, meat | Throughout day, batch at closing           | 2-5% of inventory           |
| Hawker stalls | Cooked dishes                    | End of service (2-3pm, 9-10pm)             | 5-10% of daily prep         |
| Catering      | Prepared meals, ingredients      | After events                               | 15-25% of prepared quantity |

### Current Disposal Methods

- **Incineration** (primary): Waste-to-energy plants at Tuas, Senoko, Keppel Seghers
- **Food waste digesters**: Some large establishments have on-site digesters
- **Donation**: Limited — Food from the Heart, Willing Hearts, other charities
- **Composting**: Minimal commercial adoption
- **Direct disposal**: Many small F&B operators use general waste collection

**Cost of disposal**: S$200-500/month for small-medium F&B establishments (waste collection fees + potential NEA surcharges for high-volume generators)

## 3. Regulatory Environment

### NEA (National Environment Agency) Regulations

**Mandatory Food Waste Reporting** (effective 2021):

- Large food waste generators (hotels, malls, large F&B) must report waste quantities
- Required to implement food waste reduction measures
- Applies to: Hotels with >200 rooms, malls with >10,000 sqm, large industrial facilities
- Small F&B establishments currently exempt — but regulations are tightening

**Waste Management Framework**:

- Resource Sustainability Act (2019) — framework for waste reduction targets
- Singapore Green Plan 2030 includes food waste reduction targets
- NEA provides guidelines for food waste segregation

### SFA (Singapore Food Agency) Regulations on Surplus Food Sales

**Key legal position**: Selling near-expiry or surplus food is **legal** in Singapore provided:

1. **Use-by date vs. Best-before date distinction**:
   - **Use-by date**: Food safety critical — selling past use-by date is ILLEGAL
   - **Best-before date**: Quality indicator only — selling past best-before is LEGAL if food is safe
   - Our platform must enforce this distinction rigorously

2. **Food safety requirements**:
   - Food must be fit for human consumption
   - Proper storage and handling must be maintained
   - Temperature control requirements for perishable items
   - Merchant bears responsibility for food safety

3. **Labeling requirements**:
   - Clear indication of best-before/use-by dates
   - Any modifications to original packaging must retain required labels
   - Discounted pricing must be clearly communicated

4. **Licensing**:
   - Merchants must hold valid SFA food establishment license
   - Platform should verify license status during onboarding
   - Online food sales fall under existing SFA regulations

## 4. Consumer Behavior

### Attitudes Toward Surplus Food

- **65% of Singapore consumers** willing to purchase near-expiry food at 30-50% discount (NEA survey)
- **Younger demographics (18-35)** most receptive — sustainability + budget consciousness
- **Older demographics (50+)** more skeptical about food safety concerns
- **Trust signals critical**: Brand reputation, food safety certifications, clear labeling

### Price Sensitivity

- Singapore's cost of living pressures increasing consumer price sensitivity
- Average household food expenditure: S$800-1,200/month
- F&B inflation running 5-7% annually (2023-2024)
- Growing demand for "value dining" options
- Hawker culture provides baseline affordability expectation — platform must compete on convenience + variety

### Sustainability Consciousness

- **73% of Singapore consumers** say they care about sustainability (but behavior gap exists)
- **38% actively take steps** to reduce personal food waste
- Gamification and impact tracking can bridge intention-action gap
- Social proof and community norms drive adoption

### Mobile App Usage

- **96% smartphone penetration** in Singapore
- **Food delivery app usage**: 78% of adults use food delivery apps (GrabFood, foodpanda, Deliveroo)
- **Payment preferences**: PayNow (72%), credit cards (65%), GrabPay (45%), Apple/Google Pay (30%)
- High comfort with mobile transactions — low adoption barrier

### ML Development Phases

1. **Phase 1 (Launch)**: Rule-based + simple statistical models (moving averages, basic collaborative filtering)
2. **Phase 2 (3-6 months)**: Classical ML models (XGBoost for prediction, matrix factorization for recommendations)
3. **Phase 3 (6-12 months)**: Deep learning models (LSTM for time-series, neural collaborative filtering)
4. **Phase 4 (12+ months)**: Advanced capabilities (reinforcement learning for pricing, graph neural networks for recommendations)

## Key Takeaways for Platform Design

1. **Regulatory tailwind**: Government is actively pushing food waste reduction — policy alignment is strong
2. **Legal clarity**: Selling near-expiry food is legal (with safety caveats) — no regulatory blocker
3. **Market gap**: No ML-powered competitor in Singapore — first-mover advantage possible
4. **Consumer readiness**: High smartphone penetration, growing price sensitivity, and sustainability awareness
5. **Data availability**: Government data + platform data sufficient for initial ML models
6. **Critical constraint**: Must enforce use-by vs. best-before distinction rigorously — food safety is non-negotiable
7. **Hawker inclusion**: Singapore's unique hawker culture represents both opportunity (high surplus volumes) and challenge (digital adoption barriers)
